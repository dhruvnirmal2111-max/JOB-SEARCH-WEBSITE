## Concept

A **distribution** describes how likely each value of a variable is. Three you must know cold:

- **Normal (Gaussian):** the bell curve. Symmetric, defined by mean (μ) and standard deviation (σ). ~68% of values fall within 1σ of the mean, ~95% within 2σ, ~99.7% within 3σ (the "68-95-99.7 rule"). Shows up everywhere because of the theorem below.
- **Binomial:** count of successes in *n* independent yes/no trials each with probability *p* (e.g. how many of 100 visitors convert). Mean = np.
- **Poisson:** count of events in a fixed window when events are rare and independent (e.g. support tickets per hour). One parameter, λ (the average rate).

The **Central Limit Theorem (CLT)** is the reason the normal distribution is everywhere:

> If you take many independent samples and average them, the distribution of those **sample means** is approximately **normal** — no matter what shape the original data has — as the sample size grows (rule of thumb: n ≥ 30).

That's the load-bearing idea behind confidence intervals and most A/B tests: even if your raw data is skewed (revenue per user is very skewed), the *average* behaves normally, so you can reason about it with normal-based maths.

The spread of that distribution of sample means has its own name: the **standard error** = σ / √n. Notice it shrinks as n grows — more data, more precise estimate of the mean.

## Worked example

You measure session length on Linktree. Raw session lengths are heavily right-skewed (most short, a few very long) — definitely not normal.

You take a sample of n = 400 sessions and compute the **mean** session length = 90 seconds, with sample σ = 120 seconds.

- Standard error = 120 / √400 = 120 / 20 = **6 seconds**.
- By the CLT, the sample mean is ~normal, so a 95% confidence interval is roughly mean ± 2·SE = 90 ± 12 → **[78, 102] seconds**.

So even though individual sessions are wildly skewed, you can still say "the true average session length is very likely between 78 and 102 seconds." That's the CLT doing the work.

## Interview angle

- **"Why can we use normal-based tests when our data isn't normal?"** → Because of the CLT: the *test statistic* is based on the sample mean/difference of means, which is approximately normal for a large enough sample, regardless of the raw data's shape.
- **Trap:** the CLT is about the distribution of the **sample mean**, NOT the raw data. Don't say "the data becomes normal with more samples" — the data keeps its shape; the *means* are normal.
- **When it breaks:** tiny samples, extremely heavy tails/outliers, or non-independent observations. Then reach for non-parametric tests or bootstrapping.
- Know the **68-95-99.7 rule** and **SE = σ/√n** by heart — they come up constantly.

## Self-check
1. Your raw data is very skewed. You take samples of size 50 and plot the sample means. What shape do they take, and why?
2. Standard error is σ/√n. If you want to halve your standard error, how much more data do you need?
3. In one sentence, what's the difference between standard deviation and standard error?

## Answers
1. Approximately **normal**, by the CLT — averaging washes out the skew as long as n is reasonably large (~30+) and observations are independent.
2. **4× the data.** SE scales with 1/√n, so to halve SE you need √n to double, i.e. n to quadruple.
3. **Standard deviation** measures spread of the *raw data*; **standard error** measures spread of a *sample statistic* (like the mean) — how precisely you've estimated it. SE = SD/√n.
