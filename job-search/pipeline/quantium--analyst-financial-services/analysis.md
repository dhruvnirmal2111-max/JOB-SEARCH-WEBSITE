## 4. Role-Specific Interview Preparation

### Category 1: Problem Framing and Consulting Judgement

These questions probe the core consulting competency — the ability to take a messy, undefined problem and turn it into a structured analytical plan. This is what Quantium values most at Analyst level.

**Likely questions:**

1. "Walk me through a time you received an ambiguous brief and had to decide what the actual question was before you could start the analysis."

2. "A client comes to you and says 'our costs seem high but we're not sure where.' How do you structure your approach?"

3. "How do you decide what analysis not to do? How do you prioritise when you could go in five directions?"

4. "Tell me about a time your analysis led to a different question than the one you started with."

5. "How do you balance getting to the answer quickly versus getting it right?"

**How to answer — drawing on your projects:**

For Question 1: Use P3. The restaurant chain came to Dhruv with a vague concern about costs. The brief was not "build a pricing-decision dashboard" — it was "we think we're losing money somewhere." The analytical work was in structuring that into: which categories? which suppliers? price movement or volume movement? That framing work is the answer, not the dashboard build.

For Question 2: This is almost exactly P3 + P18. Name the framework you used: decompose cost into price × volume, isolate the categories with the highest absolute spend, then look for anomalies within those. The structure, not the tools, is what they are testing.

For Question 3: Use the Pareto principle you applied in P6. With A$12B of spend, the analytical decision was to manually categorise the high-value head (high accuracy, high impact) and use ML for the long tail (speed). That is a real example of deciding what analysis gets which level of rigour.

**Key point:** At Analyst level, Quantium is not testing technical depth in these questions. They are testing whether you can think before you compute. Demonstrate that you frame first, then analyse.

### Category 3: Technical Coding (SQL and Python)

Quantium is unlikely to run algorithmic LeetCode-style interviews at Analyst level. The technical questions will be applied: given a dataset, how would you approach this analytical problem? Expect SQL and Python questions framed around real analytical scenarios, not data structure problems.

**Likely questions:**

1. "Given a table of insurance claims (claim ID, policy ID, date, amount, claim type, status), write a SQL query to find policies with an unusually high claims frequency in the past 12 months compared to their historical average."

2. "You have a dataset of bank transactions (customer ID, transaction date, amount, merchant category). Walk me through how you would identify anomalous spending patterns that might indicate fraud."

3. "In Python, how would you build a simple churn prediction model for a superannuation fund given member contribution history and engagement data?"

4. "You have a dataset where 95% of records are 'no claim' and 5% are 'claim.' How do you handle this in a classification model?"

5. "What is the difference between precision and recall, and in a fraud detection context, which would you optimise for and why?"

**How to prepare:**

For SQL: Your production SQL (P18 multi-region platform, P1 ETL pipelines) covers the fundamentals. Practice writing window functions (LAG, LEAD, running averages) and GROUP BY aggregations in an insurance/banking context. HackerRank SQL track (hackerrank.com/domains/sql) — do the "Advanced" section, 1 hour.

For the class-imbalance question: You have done this in P6 (explicit class-imbalance handling in the spend-categorisation model). Name the techniques: stratified sampling, SMOTE, class-weight adjustment, threshold tuning, and why you chose what you chose.

For precision/recall: In fraud detection, false negatives (missing fraud) are more costly than false positives (flagging legitimate transactions), so you optimise recall. In insurance claims triage, depends on business context. Practise articulating this tradeoff fluently — it is a near-certain question.

**Practice resources:**
- HackerRank SQL (free, hackerrank.com/domains/sql) — 1-2 hours
- LeetCode "Database" tag easy/medium problems (free, leetcode.com) — for window function practice
- Kaggle "Give Me Some Credit" or "Home Credit Default Risk" datasets for Python imbalanced classification practice — free

4. You commit to a realistic ramp ("productive within a week") — not overselling, not underselling

**What to avoid:**
- "I'm happy to learn R" (sounds passive; everyone says this)
- Claiming R experience you don't have
- Being defensive or over-explaining the gap
- Waiting to be asked — name it proactively if it comes up in screening

**Preparation requirement:** Do the 7-hour ramp plan in Gap 1 before your first interview. You will not be fluent, but you will have run real R code — which makes the claim above true, not aspirational.
